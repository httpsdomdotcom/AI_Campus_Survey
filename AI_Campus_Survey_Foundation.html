AI on Campus: A National Two-Layer Survey
Mapping how students and staff use AI tools across U.S. universities to guide institutional policy development, equity frameworks, and funding priorities.
The Problem
Why this survey needs to exist

AI tools have already been absorbed into how students learn and how the modern workforce operates. Yet most universities are making real-time enforcement decisions with no data and no shared standard. Is submitting a grammar-checked paragraph through ChatGPT allowed? What about restructuring an argument? Generating an outline to start from? Each instructor decides differently, each department signals something different, and students are left navigating ambiguity that can carry real academic consequences.


The issue is not that AI exists on campus. It is that no clear framework exists to distinguish between acceptable assistance and academic dishonesty, at a moment when AI is not going away and is already expected in the workplace. This survey generates the first structured, cross-institutional dataset to bridge that gap and give universities the evidence they need to act.

Survey Identity
Scope
National across U.S. universities, piloting at UC Berkeley as the launch institution
Primary audience
All undergraduate students broadly, plus university staff and faculty across departments
End goal
Policy structure guidance for administrators, and funding case for AI access and literacy programs
Survey Architecture
Two parallel layers, one unified dataset

The survey runs as two distinct instruments that feed into a shared published report. Both layers ask about the same underlying reality from different vantage points. The gap between those perspectives is where the most actionable policy tension lives, and it is the core data story this project is built to surface.


Student responses reveal ground-level behavior and unmet clarity needs. Staff and faculty responses reveal enforcement challenges, tolerance thresholds, and what a workable policy would actually require. Together they produce a picture no single-layer survey can generate.

Survey Layers
Layer 1 — Student
Student Layer
Targets all undergraduate students broadly, regardless of major or year. Captures ground-level usage behavior, attitudes toward current policies, and unmet clarity needs.
01Which AI tools are you currently using? (ChatGPT, Claude, Grammarly, Copilot, Perplexity, etc.)
02What are you primarily using AI for? (grammar/editing, brainstorming, research, outline generation, full drafts, coding, etc.)
03How often do you use AI tools in an academic context per week?
04Does your institution have clear, consistent guidelines on what AI use is and is not acceptable?
05Do current policies feel realistic given how AI is used in the workforce you are entering?
06Have you ever been uncertain whether a specific AI use was allowed under your course policy?
07Do you have equal access to AI tools compared to your peers? (cost, device access, awareness)
08What type of institutional AI guidance would be most useful to you?
09Would you support a university-wide AI use framework with clearly defined tiers of acceptable use?
Layer 2 — Staff and Faculty
Staff and Faculty Layer
Targets instructors, TAs, advisors, and academic staff. Captures enforcement challenges, tolerance thresholds, policy preferences, and institutional support gaps.
01Are you using AI tools in your own academic, research, or professional work?
02How are you currently addressing AI use in your courses or role? (syllabus policy, honor code reference, case-by-case, none)
03Does your institution provide clear guidance to faculty on how to handle student AI use?
04What level of AI assistance do you consider acceptable in student submissions? (none / grammar only / brainstorming / collaborative / open with disclosure)
05Under what circumstances would you want AI use permitted in your course, and what would you require from students?
06How confident are you in your ability to detect AI-generated content in student work?
07Are you concerned about equity in AI tool access among your students?
08What would a useful, enforceable institutional AI policy look like from your perspective?
09Would you support tiered AI use policies that vary by course type or assignment format?
Core Research Questions
What the data needs to answer
R1What is the actual gap between how students use AI and what staff believe or prefer?
R2What specific AI use cases do staff want to permit, and under what conditions?
R3Are current policies creating inequity through unequal AI tool access or awareness?
R4Is there institutional support infrastructure in place, or are individuals navigating this alone?
R5What does a tiered, enforceable, campus-wide AI policy framework actually need to include?
Intended Outputs
What this survey feeds into
Published report / white paper
Cross-institutional AI usage data presented as a policy-ready document for university administrators, academic leaders, and higher education policy groups.
Policy framework template
A tiered AI use guideline structure universities can adopt or adapt, grounded in what students and staff actually reported wanting and needing.
Funding case
A data-backed argument for institutional investment in AI tool access, AI literacy programs, and ongoing policy development resources.
Pitch assets
Deck and one-pager for presenting findings to university departments, technology sponsors, research bodies, and higher education policy stakeholders.
