AI on Campus

A national two-layer survey mapping how students and faculty use AI tools across U.S. universities — to build the evidence base for equitable, enforceable institutional policy.

The Problem

Why this survey needs to exist

AI tools have already been absorbed into how students learn and how the modern workforce operates. Yet most universities are making real-time enforcement decisions with no data and no shared standard. Is submitting a grammar-checked paragraph through ChatGPT allowed? What about restructuring an argument? Generating an outline to start from? Each instructor decides differently, each department signals something different, and students are left navigating ambiguity that can carry real academic consequences.

The issue is not that AI exists on campus. It is that no clear framework exists to distinguish between acceptable assistance and academic dishonesty — at a moment when AI is not going away and is already expected in the workplace. This survey generates the first structured, cross-institutional dataset to bridge that gap and give universities the evidence they need to act.

What We're Mapping

The core questions driving this research

The survey orbits a set of tensions that every university is quietly managing right now — between enforcement and access, between academic integrity and workforce preparation, between what students are actually doing and what institutions believe or prefer.

Survey Architecture

Two parallel layers, one unified dataset

The survey runs as two distinct instruments that feed into a shared published report. Student responses reveal ground-level behavior and unmet clarity needs. Staff and faculty responses reveal enforcement challenges, tolerance thresholds, and what a workable policy would actually require. The gap between those perspectives is where the most actionable policy tension lives.

Layer 1 — Student
Student Layer

All undergraduate students, regardless of major or year. Captures ground-level usage, attitudes toward current policies, and unmet clarity needs.

  • 01Which AI tools are you currently using?
  • 02What are you primarily using AI for?
  • 03How often do you use AI in an academic context per week?
  • 04Does your institution have clear, consistent AI guidelines?
  • 05Do current policies feel realistic given workforce expectations?
  • 06Have you ever been uncertain whether a specific AI use was allowed?
  • 07Do you have equal access to AI tools compared to peers?
  • 08What type of institutional AI guidance would help most?
  • 09Would you support a tiered university-wide AI use framework?
Layer 2 — Staff & Faculty
Staff & Faculty Layer

Instructors, TAs, advisors, and academic staff. Captures enforcement challenges, tolerance thresholds, policy preferences, and institutional support gaps.

  • 01Are you using AI tools in your own academic or professional work?
  • 02How are you currently addressing AI use in your courses?
  • 03Does your institution provide clear guidance on student AI use?
  • 04What level of AI assistance do you consider acceptable in submissions?
  • 05Under what circumstances would you want AI use permitted?
  • 06How confident are you detecting AI-generated content?
  • 07Are you concerned about equity in AI tool access among students?
  • 08What would a useful, enforceable AI policy look like to you?
  • 09Would you support tiered AI policies that vary by course type?
Research Questions

What the data needs to answer

  • R1 What is the actual gap between how students use AI and what staff believe or prefer?
  • R2 What specific AI use cases do staff want to permit, and under what conditions?
  • R3 Are current policies creating inequity through unequal AI tool access or awareness?
  • R4 Is there institutional support infrastructure in place, or are individuals navigating this alone?
  • R5 What does a tiered, enforceable, campus-wide AI policy framework actually need to include?
Intended Outputs

What this survey feeds into

  • Published report & white paper
    Cross-institutional AI usage data presented as a policy-ready document for university administrators, academic leaders, and higher education policy groups.
  • Policy framework template
    A tiered AI use guideline structure universities can adopt or adapt, grounded in what students and staff actually reported wanting and needing.
  • Equity & access brief
    A focused analysis of disparities in AI tool access across student populations, surfacing where institutions need to close gaps before policies can be fairly enforced.
